The Catalyst

Ghana's "No Bed Syndrome" refers to the chronic shortage of hospital beds in Ghana, where patients, especially in emergencies, are turned away due to a lack of capacity or "no beds." This then leads to deaths and substandard care, such as treatment on floors or benches.

Understanding the Catalyst

Charles Amissah, a 29-year-old engineer hit in a 2026 Accra crash, was taken to and denied at three hospitals: Police Hospital (no space), Ridge Hospital (Greater Accra Regional Hospital), and Korle Bu Teaching Hospital, where he was advised to continue on to University of Ghana Medical Centre instead. After roughly three hours in transit without receiving care, he died in the ambulance. Despite the shock surrounding his case, Ghana's "no bed syndrome" long predates Amissah's death. In 2017, the widely publicized death of Vivian Dzifa Bampoe Addo, a young woman reportedly turned away by multiple hospitals while in critical condition, sparked national outrage and forced public attention onto the issue. Yet even after that moment of visibility, similar stories continued to surface. A 70-year-old man in 2018 was rejected by several facilities and died in his car. A pregnant woman lost both her life and her baby after being denied timely emergency care. Countless unnamed patients have been left waiting on benches, floors, or in overcrowded corridors until help came too late.

Taken together, these cases show that these deaths are not simply tragic accidents but recurring outcomes produced by the same architecture of scarcity and neglect. Underfunded public hospitals, chronic staff and bed shortages, fragile referral systems, and a political class that seems to only respond when a story briefly trends all contribute to a system where emergency care becomes uncertain rather than guaranteed. In that sense, Amissah's death is not an exception but part of an ongoing death toll that has been quietly accumulating for years. "No bed" begins to function less as a temporary excuse and more as shorthand for a healthcare system that has already begun to determine whose lives can be treated as expendable.
